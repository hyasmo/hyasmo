A decision guide for K–12 educators — use this before incorporating any AI tool into lesson planning, assessment design, or student-facing work.

⚠ Pause first
Check your data policy
Before using any AI tool with student data, verify: Is this tool FERPA/COPPA compliant? Has your district approved it? Does the vendor use student inputs to train their model? If you can't answer yes to all three — do not proceed. Use a district-approved tool or anonymize the data first.
→ Continue to Q2
Question 2 of 4
Is this task something a student will directly receive or submit as their own?
e.g. a writing prompt, a test question, feedback on their work, a rubric they'll use
Yes — student-facing
No — teacher-side only
⟳ Review before use
Human review required
AI can draft — you must verify. Check for accuracy, bias, cultural assumptions, and age-appropriateness. Ask: would I be comfortable if a parent saw this came from AI? If yes and it's accurate, use it. If not, revise or discard.
✓ Lower risk
AI as a planning tool
For teacher-side tasks (lesson outlines, pacing guides, differentiation ideas, admin drafts), AI is lower-risk. Still verify factual claims before acting on them. Treat AI output as a first draft, not a final product.

↺ Redesign the task
The assignment needs a HEART check
If AI can do it, the task may not be measuring the right thing. Consider: Human context, Evidence of process, Argumentation, Real audience, Transfer. Add at least one element AI cannot replicate — lived experience, in-class discussion, revision history.
✓ Good design
This task is AI-resilient
Your assignment already requires genuine thinking that AI cannot shortcut. Document what makes it resilient — this is good design practice and useful if the question ever comes up.

The HEART Framework
For AI-resilient assignment design, build in at least one of:
  • Human context — student's own experience
  • Evidence of process — drafts, annotations
  • Argumentation — defend and revise positions
  • Real audience — beyond the teacher
  • Transfer — apply to novel situations
Data Privacy Checklist
Before any AI tool touches student data:
  • FERPA / COPPA compliant?
  • District-approved?
  • No student data used for model training?
  • Clear data retention policy?
  • When in doubt — anonymise or don't use
AI as a Thinking Partner
Four patterns for using AI to deepen — not shortcut — student thinking:
  • The Challenger — argue against my thesis
  • The First Drafter — students critique & revise
  • The Research Assistant — suggest, then verify
  • The Socratic Bot — ask questions only
