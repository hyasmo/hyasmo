Instructional Design Sample · Micro-Course

Teaching with AI
A Micro-Course for
K–12 Educators

DesignerHana Morsi · hyasmo
AudienceK–12 Educators, all subjects
FormatAsynchronous, LMS-deliverable
Duration3 modules · 9 lessons · ~4–6 hours
Credit0.5 CEU · Portfolio-based assessment
FrameworksADDIE · UDL · Bloom's · TPACK · Kirkpatrick

Design Philosophy

"Good learning design is empathic, user-centered, and uncomplicated in its path to learning."
— Hana Morsi · hyasmo

Course Overview

Teaching with AI is a professional development micro-course designed to build meaningful, ethical AI fluency in K–12 educators. Unlike tool-focused trainings, this course is anchored in pedagogy: every module asks not just "what can AI do?" but "what should you do with it, and why?"

Course Outcomes

Evaluate AI tools for classroom-appropriateness using the HEART decision framework
Identify and mitigate risks related to student data privacy, bias, and academic integrity
Apply a pedagogy-first lens — selecting tools that serve learning goals, not replace them
Design at least one AI-resilient lesson requiring genuine student thinking
Use AI as a thinking partner for lesson planning, differentiation, and feedback
Articulate a personal classroom AI use policy written for students and families

Module 01

Module 01
AI Tool Literacy
~90 minutes
0.15 CEU

Educators cannot make good decisions about AI tools they don't understand. This module builds foundational, jargon-free literacy — not to make teachers into technologists, but to give them enough conceptual grounding to evaluate, question, and choose wisely.

Lesson 1.1
What Is Generative AI, Actually?
~25 min
Bloom's Level
Remember / Understand
Format
Explainer video (8 min) + interactive vocabulary builder + reflection prompt
Content
Demystifies how large language models work without requiring technical background. Uses the metaphor of a very well-read autocomplete. Covers: what AI "sees," how it predicts outputs, what hallucination means and why it happens.
Key Concept
AI does not know things — it predicts statistically likely text. This distinction changes everything about how you use it.
Lesson 1.2
Navigating the AI Tool Landscape
~30 min
Bloom's Level
Understand / Analyze
Format
Comparative tool overview + categorization activity + discussion board
Content
Surveys the current landscape: general tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot), education-specific tools (Magic School AI, Curipod, Diffit), and red-flag categories — tools with unclear data policies or student-facing tools without FERPA compliance. Learners categorize a set of tools using provided criteria.
Assessment
Scenario quiz: given a description of a task and a set of tool options, select the most appropriate tool and explain your reasoning.
Lesson 1.3
The Decision Framework: Should I Use AI for This?
~35 min
Bloom's Level
Apply
Format
Walkthrough of Job Aid + four real-world scenarios + portfolio entry
Content
Introduces the "Should I Use AI for This?" decision guide. Educators work through four scenarios representing common use cases: lesson planning, feedback generation, quiz creation, and student support. Each requires a documented decision and rationale.
Portfolio Entry
Learners submit a completed decision guide for one real task from their upcoming teaching week.

Module 02

Module 02
AI Ethics in Education
~100 minutes
0.18 CEU

AI is not neutral. This module takes educators beyond "is this tool safe?" to ask deeper questions about power, representation, and responsibility. It is the most challenging module by design — discomfort is part of the learning.

Lesson 2.1
Bias, Representation, and Algorithmic Justice
~35 min
Bloom's Level
Analyze / Evaluate
Format
Case study analysis + guided discussion + reflection prompt
Content
Examines documented cases of AI bias in educational contexts: biased grading systems, representation gaps in training data, cultural assumptions baked into language models. Educators analyze a real AI-generated piece of feedback and identify potential bias vectors.
Discussion
"Describe a student in your classroom whose identity or experience might be misrepresented by an AI tool. What would you need to verify before using that tool with them?"
Lesson 2.2
Student Data Privacy: FERPA, COPPA, and Beyond
~30 min
Bloom's Level
Understand / Apply
Format
Explainer + interactive compliance checklist + scenario practice
Content
Breaks down FERPA and COPPA in plain language. Covers: what counts as student data, what "model training" means for privacy, how to read a tool's privacy policy, and what "district-approved" actually means. Learners practice evaluating two tool privacy policies.
Lesson 2.3
Academic Integrity in the Age of AI
~35 min
Bloom's Level
Evaluate / Create
Format
Policy analysis + position statement + peer exchange
Content
Moves beyond "is this cheating?" to "what do we actually value in student work, and does AI threaten it?" Introduces the HEART framework as a design tool for AI-resilient assignments. Educators analyze their school AI policy and draft a classroom-level position statement.
Portfolio Entry
Draft a one-paragraph AI use policy for your classroom, written for students and their families.

Module 03

Module 03
Pedagogy First
~110 minutes
0.17 CEU

The final module is where everything comes together. Educators move from understanding and evaluating AI to actively redesigning their practice with it. The emphasis throughout is on AI as a partner to human judgment, not a replacement for it.

Lesson 3.1
AI as a Professional Tool
~35 min
Bloom's Level
Apply
Format
Live-practice module (learner uses AI in real time) + reflection protocol
Content
Educators choose one of their own upcoming lessons and use an AI tool to: generate a differentiated version for one learner population, draft discussion questions, and create an exit ticket. They evaluate each output and document what they kept, revised, and discarded.
Key Principle
AI produces a first draft. You produce the final product. The gap between them is your professional judgment.
Lesson 3.2
Designing AI-Resilient Assignments
~40 min
Bloom's Level
Create
Format
Framework application + peer review + revision
HEART Framework
Human context · Evidence of process · Argumentation · Real audience · Transfer
Content
Educators take an existing assignment and run it through the HEART checklist. They identify which elements are present, which are absent, and redesign at least one component. Peer exchange using a structured rubric follows.
Lesson 3.3
AI as a Student Thinking Partner
~35 min
Bloom's Level
Evaluate / Synthesize
Format
Strategy overview + classroom scenario analysis + portfolio completion
Four Patterns
The Challenger — AI argues against the student's position · The First Drafter — students critique and revise AI output · The Research Assistant — AI suggests, students verify · The Socratic Bot — AI may only ask questions
Portfolio Entry
Select one pattern to pilot with students. Document your implementation plan and anticipated challenges.

Evaluation Strategy · Kirkpatrick Model

Level Focus Method Timing
Level 1
Reaction		 Did learners find the training relevant and engaging? Post-lesson reflection prompts; end-of-module satisfaction survey End of each lesson and module
Level 2
Learning		 Did learners acquire the intended knowledge and skills? Scenario-based knowledge checks per module; vocabulary assessments End of each module
Level 3
Behavior		 Did learners change their practice? Final portfolio: redesigned lesson, AI policy, and decision guide End of course
Level 4
Results		 Did the training impact student outcomes? 30-day follow-up survey: classroom implementation, perceived impact 30 days post-completion

Design Frameworks

ADDIE
Analyze · Design · Develop · Implement · Evaluate
The foundational ID framework structuring this course's development lifecycle.
UDL
Universal Design for Learning
Multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression throughout all modules.
Bloom's
Bloom's Taxonomy
Learning objectives span all six levels; each lesson's assessment matches its stated level.
TPACK
Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge
Technology integration anchored in content and pedagogical knowledge — not tool-first.
Constructivism
Social Constructivism · Critical Pedagogy
Learners build understanding through active engagement, reflection, and peer exchange.
Kirkpatrick
Four-Level Evaluation Model
Evaluation spans reaction through results, with a 30-day follow-up to measure transfer.
