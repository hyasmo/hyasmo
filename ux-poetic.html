An essay on design + language

Can UX Be
Poetic?

Good design, like good poetry, makes you feel something — and trusts you to arrive at the meaning yourself.

Read
I

The problem with
explaining yourself

Most interfaces over-explain. They label the obvious, confirm the understood, and narrate every action back to you as though you might have forgotten what you just did. This is not clarity — it is anxiety dressed as helpfulness.

Poetry works differently. A poem does not tell you how to feel. It arranges words in a way that makes feeling inevitable. The meaning arrives in you, not from the text.

Good UX does the same. It creates the conditions for understanding rather than delivering understanding pre-packaged. The difference is enormous — one treats the user as a reader, the other as a passenger.

Poetry is the art of saying exactly what you need to — and nothing more. That precision, that economy of language, is the same standard great design holds itself to.

— On the shared grammar of design and verse

II

Brevity is not
the enemy of depth

There is a persistent myth in design that simplicity means emptiness — that reducing friction requires reducing meaning. That to make something easy to use, you must sand away everything interesting about it.

Poetry dismantles this completely. A haiku carries more weight per word than most novels. Compression does not reduce meaning — it concentrates it. The white space around a line is not absence. It is part of the poem.

The same is true of interfaces. The space between elements is not wasted. The thing you choose not to say is a design decision. Restraint, when intentional, is its own kind of eloquence.

What poetry does
Trusts the reader to complete the meaning
Uses white space as part of the work
Says one true thing precisely
Makes you feel before you understand
Rewards attention
What good UX does
Trusts the user to complete the action
Uses negative space as part of the layout
Communicates one clear thing per moment
Makes you feel oriented before you think
Rewards exploration
III

Design that
moves you

We talk about UX in the language of efficiency — flows, friction, conversion, drop-off. These are real things that matter. But they are not the whole picture.

There are interfaces that do their job and interfaces that leave something with you. The ones that linger are the ones that had a point of view. They made a choice about how something should feel, not just how it should work.

That choice — that commitment to a particular emotional register — is where design and poetry meet. It is the decision to make something that doesn't just function but resonates. That is harder than it sounds. Most things that work don't resonate. Most things that resonate don't scale. The overlap is small, and worth chasing.

The best interfaces, like the best poems, say exactly what they need to and nothing more — and somehow, that's when they move you.

— Hana Morsi

IV

So — can it?

Yes. But not by trying to be poetic. An interface that reaches for poetry becomes precious, self-conscious, difficult. That is not poetry — that is affectation.

UX becomes poetic the same way a sentence becomes beautiful — by being exactly right. By saying what it needs to say in the way that serves the reader best, and then stopping. By trusting that the person on the other side is intelligent, capable, and deserving of something considered.

That is the standard. Not beauty for its own sake. Not cleverness. Not decoration. Exactness. Intention. The courage to leave things out.

That is where design and poetry share a home.
